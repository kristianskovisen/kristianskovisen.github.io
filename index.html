Product Relatedness in Tasks: A Novel Measure and Multi-Product Firms

Products vary in the tasks they require and, consequently, in the occupations they draw on. I develop a novel measure of product relatedness grounded in the occupational composition of single-product manufacturers in Denmark. Using matched employer–employee data, I represent each product as a vector of task requirements and construct a similarity measure that combines occupational overlap with skill transferability inferred from worker flows. To take the measure to multi-product firms, I anchor each firm to its core product and compute the task distance between that core and every candidate product, linking these distances to the firm's product-scope decisions. Multi-product firms expand into products closely related to their core and drop products that are more distant in task space—consistent with rising marginal costs of producing away from core competencies. These findings highlight the role of workforce composition in shaping firm scope and offer a task-based perspective on diversification.
